Sister act

Big sister, little sister, red sister: three woman at the heart of twentieth-century China.

Once upon a time, the story goes, there were there sisters. One loved money, one loved power, and one loved her country.

…

The extraordinary lives of the soongs were made possible by a confluence of globalization, modernization and Western penetration of an ancient, tottering empire.

…

For her grace and magnetism, May-ling was a woman fatally addicted to luxury, and EI-ling spent much of her life playing politics to maintain an opulent lifestyle for the Soong clan.

…

As China struggled to define itself against the Western world, the sisters were discovering how to be a new kind of Chinese: cos-mopolitian, enlightened and, most unprecedented for a woman, liberated.

…

Ching-ling fared no better, heedlessly sacrificing herself for a revolution she didn’t understand until it was too late. Red sister might have begun life emulating joan of Arc, but he fate more closely resembled that of Dorothea Brooke.

…

Unlike in a fairy tale, there was no magical ending to the Soong saga.

Each sister made her choice early, and by the time she pasued to consider the arc of her life, the world had moved on and she was no longer in a position to walk back her youthful convictions. As Ching-ling coolly put it during her last years, in words that sound like an attempt to persuade herself as much as anyone else, “I made my choice and I have no regrets.”
